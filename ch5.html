Input Devices

Scanners

2D Scanners

  • While scanning a document:
    • Xenon lamp illuminates the document.
    • A scan head moves across the document until the page is scanned.
    • The image of the document is sent to a focusing lens through a series of mirrors.
    • Focused image is turned into an electronic form by a Charge Coupled Device (CCD).
  • Equipped with Optical Character Recognition (OCR) to turn a document into text.
  • If a photo was scanned, it is saved as a JPEG.

3D Scanners

  • Scanned images can be used in Computer Aided Design (CAD).
  • Sent to a 3D printer to produce a working model of the scanned image.
  • Computed Tomography Scanners:
    • Builds an image of an object with thin ‘slices’, which combine to form a 3D object.
    • 2D slices are represented as columns of pixels in a computer memory.
    • The solid object’s whole surface is represented digitally this way.
  • Types of imaging:
    • Computerised Tomography (CT scanners), which use X-rays.
    • Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI), which use radio frequencies.
    • Single Photon Emission Computed Tomography. (SPECT), which use gamma rays.

Barcode readers

  • Series of dark & light parallel lines of varying thickness.
  • Maximum 30 digits can be stored. (0-9).
  • Universal Product Code (UPC) version A, is utilised.
  • Guard bars are present in the centre & at the ends.
  • Read by Red laser/ Light Emitting Diode (LED).

Quick Response (QR) codes

  • Matrix of dark-filled squares.
  • Can store 7,000 digits.
  • Can store website addresses.

Digital Cameras.

  • Controlled by a microprocessor.
    • Adjusting the shutter speed.
    • Focusing & operating the flash automatically.
    • Adjusting the aperture size.
    • Adjusting image size.
    • Removing ‘red eye’.

Keyboards.

  • Connected either via USB or wireless.
  • May lead to Repetitive Strain Injury (RSI) after frequent usage.
  • Ergonomic keyboards solve this.

Pointing devices.

  • Mouse.
    • Connected via USB.
    • Uses red LEDs to detect movement.
    • Wireless connection.
  • Trackerball.
    • Less likely for a user to suffer from RSI.

Microphones.

  • Used to input sound into a computer.
  • Either built-in or connected via USB/wirelessly.
  • Voice recognition.
    • Spoken voice is converted to digital wave pattern.
  • Speech recognition.
    • More complex than voice recognition.
    • Input words spoken by a user & output on a screen.

Touchscreens.

  • Capacitive.
    • Made up of many layers of glass.
    • Creates electric fields between the glass plates.
    • Electric field changes when touched.
    • Coordinates determined by microprocessor.
    • Benefits.
      • Medium cost.
      • Good screen visibility in sunlight.
      • Allows multi-touch.
      • Very durable screen.
    • Drawbacks.
      • Allows only usage of fingers or a special stylus.
  • Infra-red.
    • Heat-sensitive.
      • Uses glass screen.
      • Needs warm object to detect input.
    • Optical.
      • Uses glass screen.
      • Uses an array of sensors in grid-formation.
      • When touched, light is blocked & X-Y coordinates determined by microprocessor.
    • Benefits.
      • Both allow multi-touch.
      • Optical also allows for gloved fingers & styluses.
      • Both have good screen durability.
    • Drawbacks.
      • Relatively expensive.
      • Heat-sensitive only allows for fingers.
      • Both have fairly good screen visibility in sunlight.
  • Resistive.
    • Upper layer polyester.
    • Bottom layer glass.
    • Completes a circuit when touched.
    • Signals are sent, which the microprocessor interprets & the coordinates are determined.
    • Benefits.
      • Relatively inexpensive.
      • Allows for gloved fingers & styluses.
    • Drawbacks.
      • Poor screen visibility in strong sunlight.
      • No multi-touch.
      • Fair screen durability.

Sensors

  • Read/Measure data.
  • Real data is Analogue (constantly changing).
  • Need Analogue to Digital Converter (ADC) to digitally interpret real data.
  • Types.
    1. Temperature.
    2. Moisture.
    3. Light.
    4. Motion.
    5. Pressure.
    6. Acoustic.
    7. Gas.
    8. pH.
    9. Magnetic fields.

    Monitoring system

    • Microprocessor only observes
    • Expects action from user.
    • Examples.
      • Vital signs for patient.
      • Pollution levels in a river.
    • Applications.
      • Burglar alarm system.

    Control system

    • Microprocessor observes.
    • Also takes action.
    • Output affects next input.
    • Examples.
      • Changing traffic lights.
      • Regulating greenhouse environment.
    • Applications.
      • Control of street lighting.
      • Anti-lock Braking Systems (ABS).
        • Uses magnetic field sensors to stop the wheels locking up on the car.
        • Happens when brakes have been applied too sharply.

Interactive whiteboards

  • Allow computer images to be displayed on whiteboard by a digital projector.
  • User can write on the whiteboard & the text/drawings are stored in an electric form.

Output Devices

Printers.

2D printers

Inkjet

  • Consists of nozzles which spray droplets of ink.
  • Prints the page character by character.
  • Technologies.
    1. Thermal bubble.
      • Heat makes the ink vaporise & form a bubble, which is then ejected onto the paper.
    2. Piezoelectric.
      • A crystal, located behind the ink reservoir, is given a charge which causes it to vibrate.
      • This vibration forces ink to be ejected onto the paper.
  • Process.
    1. Data is sent to the printer driver.
    2. Checks if the printer is able to print.
    3. Sent to the printer buffer.
    4. A sheet of paper is fed.
    5. Print head moves side to side, spraying all 4 colours in the correct amounts to produce the desired print.
    6. Paper is allowed to pass slightly, in order to print the next line.
    7. Repeated till buffer is empty.
    8. Interrupt is sent to the computer.
  • Applications.
    1. High-quality photos.
    2. Good colour printing.

Laser.

  • Uses dry powder ink (toner) & static electricity.
  • Prints the whole page at once.
  • Much faster than Inkjet printers.
  • Process.
    1. Data is sent to the printer driver.
    2. Checks if the printer is able to print.
    3. Sent to the printer buffer.
    4. A sheet of paper is fed.
    5. Printing drum is given a positive charge.
    6. A laser beam scans across, removing the charge from areas.
    7. This matches the characters from the data, & these areas are negatively charged.
    8. Drum is coated with positively charged toner.
    9. Negatively charged sheet of paper is rolled over the drum.
    10. Toner sticks to paper; forms exact copy of data.
    11. Electric charge of paper is removed to prevent it from sticking to the drum.
    12. Finally pass through a fuser, which melts the ink so it is permanently attached to the paper.
    13. A discharge amp removes all the charge from the drum.
  • Applications.
    1. Efficient in printing multiple copies of a document.
    2. Used to print high-quality flyers/posters.
    3. Used in the field of advertising.

3D output devices

  • Used to create a physical 3D product.
  • Used for 3D prototyping.
  • Applications.
    1. Making prosthetic limbs.
    2. Making parts in the aeronautical industry.
    3. Making fashion & art.

3D printer

  • Used in computer-aided design (CAD).
  • Builds an object layer by layer.
  • Use Additive manufacturing.
  • Types
    1. Direct printing.
      • Uses inkjet technology
    2. Binder printing.
      • Uses 2 passes for each of the layers.
      • First pass sprays dry powder.
      • Second pass sprays a binder.

3D cutter

  • Uses subtractive manufacturing.
  • Can also cut beyond the surface of the material & can produce intricate designs.
  • CNC machining is an example of this.

Actuators.

  • Used in control applications involving sensors and devices.
  • These are physically moving output devices.
  • Need Digital to Analogue Converter (DAC) to convert digital data into physical movements.
  • Examples:
    • Relay
    • Solenoid
    • Motor.

Loudspeakers.

  • Sound passes through a DAC & then through an amplifier.
  • Sound is produced by voltage differences vibrating a cone in the speaker housing at different frequencies & amplitudes.
  • Sampling rate.
    • Rate at which the DAC can convert digital output into analogue voltages.
    • Normally 44,100.
  • Stereo.
    • Audio output from 2 directions; right & left.
    • By default, calculations refer to stereo sound.

Monitors.

Liquid Crystal Display (LCD).

  • Front layer of the monitor is made up of liquid crystal diodes, grouped in 3s or 4s (4th one is yellow).
  • Known as pixels.
  • Backlit by Cold Cathode Fluorescent Lamp (CCFL).
  • Light is provided by 2 fluorescent tubes being the LCD screen.

Light-Emitting Diode (LED).

  • Front layer also has LCD technology.
  • Backlit by a matrix of tiny LEDs being the LCD screen.
  • Advantages over CCFL.
    1. Reaches maximum brightness immediately.
    2. Gives a whiter light; sharpens the image & makes the colours appear more vivid. (CCFL had a yellow-is tint.)
    3. Produces a brighter light; improves colour definition.
    4. Are much thinner.
    5. More reliable & more consistent.
    6. Consumes less power.

Organic Light-emitting Diode (OLED).

  • Uses organic materials to create flexible semi-conductors.
  • Sandwiched between 2 charged electrodes (metallic cathode & glass anode).
  • Gives off light when an electric field is applied.
  • Backlighting isn’t required, screens are thinner & much more flexible; LCDs aren’t needed.
  • Advantages over LEDs & CCFLs.
    1. Thinner, lighter, & more flexible.
    2. Can bee made from plastic as opposed to glass.
    3. Give off brighter light.
    4. Backlighting not required.
    5. Use much less power.
    6. Can be made into large, thin sheets.
    7. Very large field of view (~170 degrees).

Projectors.

  • Used in presentations & in multimedia applications.
  • Types.
    1. Digital Light Projectors (DLPs).
      • Contain many millions of micro mirrors on a DLP chip.
      • Image resolution depends on: number of micro mirrors & how they are arranged.
      • Can either tilt towards or away from the light source, indicating ON/OFF respectively.
      • Can switch over several thousand times peer second—creating, generally, 1024 shades of grey (a.k.a. grey scale image).
      • A bright light is passed through a colour filter & splits into its primary colours (DLPs can create ~16 million colours).
      • The states of each DLP chip are linked to the colours to form the coloured image.
      • Used for large-audience viewing.
    2. LCD projectors.
      • Older technology.
      • Beam of white light is generated.
      • Sent to a group of chromatic-coated mirrors, which reflect the light at different wavelengths, corresponding to the primary colours.
      • These coloured rays pass through 3 LCD screens, which each showing a grayscale image.
      • A special prism combines all these images to produce one full-colures image.
      • Image passes through a projector lens onto a screen.
